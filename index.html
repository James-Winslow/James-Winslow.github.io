About

The longer version.

I'm a data scientist with a background in biostatistics, public health, and mathematics. Most of my professional work lives at the intersection of analytical rigor and practical communication — building pipelines, writing SQL, automating reports, and making sure the analysis actually reaches the people who need it.

My training is in statistics and formal methods — regression, survival analysis, study design, probabilistic thinking — but my day-to-day tends toward the engineering side: getting data clean, getting it moving, and getting it understood. I care about both halves. A pipeline that nobody trusts is just as useless as a model nobody can explain.

Outside of work I'm hiking Colorado's fourteeners, reading about the history and philosophy of probability, and working on side projects that let me apply statistical thinking to things I'm genuinely curious about — mountains, sports, human behavior. I'm trying to grow in the direction of machine learning depth and applied AI, and I prefer being honest about where I am in that process over performing expertise I haven't earned yet.

How did probability become the language of modern society?

Reading

I spend my days working with data that informs decisions. But I rarely stop to ask: how did we arrive at a world where numbers are trusted to govern? This question traces the historical arc from gambling problems in the 17th century to the statistical infrastructure of modern states — and it's changing how I think about my own work.

Reading

Ian Hacking — The Emergence of Probability How probability emerged as a dual concept — epistemic and frequentist — and why that tension still matters today.
Theodore Porter — Trust in Numbers Why quantification became the language of objectivity in public life, and what that costs us in terms of judgment and nuance.
Alain Desrosières — The Politics of Large Numbers A sociological history of statistics as a tool of state governance — how categories get built and who builds them.
Lorraine Daston — Classical Probability in the Enlightenment The philosophical foundations of probability before it became a technocratic tool.

↳ Connecting to: a long-term writing project on probability & statistics

How should we reason under uncertainty?

Reading

This is the question underneath most of my statistical training. I learned frequentist methods formally, but have always been skeptical of how mechanically they get applied. Bayesians and frequentists have been arguing for a century, and the stakes are real: how you reason about evidence shapes which treatments get approved, which policies get funded, which risks get dismissed. I want to actually understand the debate, not just pick a side.

Reading

E.T. Jaynes — Probability Theory: The Logic of Science The definitive Bayesian manifesto — probability as an extension of logic, not a frequency count. Technically demanding but philosophically clarifying.
Deborah Mayo — Statistical Inference as Severe Testing A rigorous defense of error-statistical reasoning and a sharp critique of both Bayesian and naïve frequentist approaches.
Karl Popper — The Logic of Scientific Discovery Falsifiability as the criterion of scientific knowledge — the foundation for thinking about what evidence actually tests.
Richard Jeffrey — The Logic of Decision Decision theory as the practical extension of probabilistic reasoning — what to do when you can't be certain.

↳ Connecting to: statistical methods study; long-term modeling work

How do humans actually interpret evidence and risk?

Reading

Statistical evidence rarely speaks for itself. The same risk figure lands very differently depending on who is reading it, in what cultural context, and with what emotional stakes. I came to this question through public health — where the gap between what the data says and what people do with it is often enormous — and it's stayed with me.

Reading

Gerd Gigerenzer — Reckoning with Risk How statistical illiteracy affects medical decisions — and how better communication of uncertainty could save lives. Directly relevant to my public health background.
Ulrich Beck — Risk Society How modern societies organize themselves around manufactured risks — and why risk perception is always political, not just technical.
Dan Kahan — Cultural Cognition research Why people with access to the same data reach opposite conclusions — identity-protective cognition as an empirical phenomenon, not a moral failing.

↳ Connecting to: public health background; WNBA project; future writing

Can I predict when a Colorado 14er becomes accessible?

Planned

I've been hiking Colorado's fourteeners for years. Summit windows are notoriously hard to judge — snowpack, aspect, elevation, and weather patterns interact in ways that even experienced mountaineers get wrong. I want to build a probabilistic model that estimates accessibility windows for specific peaks, connecting time-series environmental data to real mountain conditions. This project sits at the intersection of everything: mountain travel, data engineering, and prediction under genuine uncertainty.

Technical threads

Time-series forecasting SNOTEL snowpack data, NOAA weather records, and elevation-adjusted temperature modeling as inputs.
Probabilistic prediction Output as a probability distribution over accessibility windows — not a single point estimate, because mountains don't give you certainty.
Data visualization Peak-by-peak condition maps; seasonal accessibility calendars; uncertainty bands that actually communicate risk.
